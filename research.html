ODonnell Lab · Yale MCD Biology

Research

We study the multitude of ways that microbes influence the behavior and physiology of their animal hosts. Using C. elegans as a model, we combine genetics, live imaging, chemical biology, and behavioral analysis to understand how animals detect and respond to the microbial world.

01

Microbes as Modulators of Host Behavior

The impact of bacteria on nervous system function has been relatively under-studied despite the fact that animals are constantly interacting with microbes. We use C. elegans and its natural gut microbiome to understand how bacteria influence animal behavior — a system uniquely amenable to high-throughput behavioral screening under defined and naturalistic conditions.

Our work established that Providencia bacteria produce tyramine in the worm intestine, which is converted to octopamine and acts directly in sensory neurons to reduce odor aversion and increase sensory preference — demonstrating that commensal bacteria can produce neuromodulators that directly reshape host behavior.

02

Sensory Neuroscience & Neural Circuits

C. elegans has a completely mapped connectome of 302 neurons, making it uniquely powerful for understanding how neural circuits encode environmental information and generate behavior. We use optogenetics, calcium imaging, and genetic tools to dissect the circuits underlying microbially-influenced behaviors.

We are particularly interested in how neuromodulators reconfigure circuit dynamics in response to changing microbial environments.

03

Chemical Ecology of Host–Microbe Interactions

Bacteria produce a vast repertoire of small molecules that can act as signals, toxins, or nutrients. In collaboration with the Schroeder lab, we use metabolomics and chemical biology to identify bioactive microbial metabolites and understand how animals detect them — whether to seek out beneficial microbes or avoid harmful ones.

This work connects molecular chemistry to the evolution of sensory systems and ecological behavior.

04

Host Physiological Response to Colonization

Beyond behavior, colonization by different bacteria triggers distinct transcriptional and physiological responses in the host. We study how the intestinal epithelium senses and responds to bacterial colonization, and how these responses feed back to influence nervous system function and animal physiology.

Understanding these connections may have broad implications for how the microbiome influences host health and disease.
