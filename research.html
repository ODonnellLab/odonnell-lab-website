Research

Laboratory of intertissue and interorganismal chemical communication

Animal nervous systems evolved in environments surrounded by microbes. The earliest animals were — and many extant species are — microbe feeders. Our lab is motivated by the thesis that chemicals from microorganisms are critical for proper animal behavior and physiology, and that these interorganismal signals played a major role in the origins of neuronal communication. We leverage the many tools available in C. elegans to identify novel mechanisms of host-microbe and inter-tissue communication, combining bacterial and worm genetics, high-throughput screening, behavioral analysis, metabolomics, and high-resolution neural activity imaging.

01

Intertissue Trafficking of Gut Metabolites

We previously showed that gut microbial production of neurotransmitters alters chemosensory behaviors in C. elegans — Providencia bacteria produce tyramine in the worm intestine, which is converted to octopamine and acts on sensory neurons to reshape olfactory preference (O'Donnell et al., Nature 2020).

Our recent work demonstrates that octopamine is subsequently glucosylated in the intestine and modified with specific fatty acids that encode information about dietary history and metabolic state. We identified conserved biosynthetic and transport factors in an intertissue pathway leading to hydrolysis of glucosylated octopamine and chemosensory behavior changes. We continue to characterize additional glucosylated microbe- and host-derived metabolites trafficked between tissues to alter neural circuit function and behavior.

02

Chemical Signal Production & Sensation

Natural microbial communities are complex, which limits traditional cell-based receptor–ligand screening approaches. We developed Flint 2.0, a platform for rapid production of complex multicopy integrated transgenic arrays in C. elegans, enabling high-throughput receptor–ligand screening in living multicellular animals. Individual cells in adult worms can be made to express different receptors and barcoded to mark cells activated by specific ligands.

Using this approach, we are identifying microbe-produced GPCR agonists with emphasis on monoamine and opioid receptor systems that regulate satiety and feeding. In collaboration with the Musser lab (Yale), we found that monoamine signaling may have an ancient origin predating the evolution of nervous systems, suggesting these signals were key interorganismal cues before their adoption in animal neurotransmission.

03

Feeding Behavior & Predator–Prey Dynamics

Intestinal colonization by tyramine-producing bacteria increases worms' association with and feeding on those same bacteria, suggesting tradeoffs between the benefits of gut colonization (nutrient-rich environment, dispersal advantage) and predation. Microbes can survive in the C. elegans intestine and be dispersed via surface contact or defecation.

We are studying how feeding is altered by chemical signals from microbes and how host foraging shapes bacterial dispersal and community dynamics. In collaboration with the Brown lab (ICL), we screened ~100 nematode-associated bacterial strains using high-throughput locomotion analysis and identified behavioral signatures spanning diverse bacterial taxa. We use this collection to study predation dynamics, associative learning, and bacterial dispersal — and extend our findings to microbe-feeding Tetrahymena protists in collaboration with the Yan lab (Yale).
