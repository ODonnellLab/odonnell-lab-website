Research

Laboratory of intertissue and interorganismal chemical communication

From the earliest multicellular organisms to modern animals, life has never existed in isolation from microbes — and for many animals, microbes are the primary food source. We think this deep evolutionary history means that chemical signals from microorganisms are fundamental to nervous system function, and may have shaped the very origins of neuronal communication. To explore this, we use C. elegans as a genetically tractable model, combining worm and bacterial genetics, high-throughput behavioral screening, metabolomics, and live neural imaging to uncover how gut microbes and host tissues communicate chemically.

01

Intertissue Trafficking of Gut Metabolites

When Providencia bacteria colonize the C. elegans gut, they synthesize tyramine, which the worm converts to the neuromodulator octopamine. Elevated octopamine in sensory neurons shifts how the animal perceives odors — directly coupling bacterial metabolism to host behavior (O'Donnell et al., Nature 2020).

Gut-derived neurotransmitters don't stay local. We found that octopamine undergoes sugar conjugation in the intestine and is then modified with fatty acids that reflect the worm's recent dietary history — creating a chemical record that travels between tissues and is ultimately decoded by the nervous system. We are now mapping the broader landscape of glucosylated metabolites, from both microbial and host sources, that move through this intertissue route to influence sensory decisions and behavior.

02

Chemical Signal Production & Sensation

Pinpointing which microbial molecules activate specific host receptors is difficult in complex natural communities. To address this, we developed Flint 2.0 — a tool for generating C. elegans carrying stably integrated, cell-barcoded receptor libraries. Each cell in the worm expresses a distinct GPCR and fluoresces when its cognate ligand binds, enabling receptor–ligand screens to be run directly in a living animal rather than in cultured cells.

We are using this platform to identify microbial activators of monoamine and opioid receptors, both of which govern hunger and food-seeking. In a collaboration with the Musser lab (Yale), we found monoamine signaling operating in sponges — animals without a nervous system — raising the possibility that these pathways originally evolved as cross-species chemical communication before being co-opted by neurons.

03

Feeding Behavior & Predator–Prey Dynamics

Bacteria that colonize the C. elegans gut gain access to a warm, nutrient-rich environment and a mobile host that can carry and spread them. But the worm is also a predator. We found that colonization by tyramine-producing bacteria makes worms more likely to seek out and feed on that same strain — a feedback loop with consequences for both host behavior and bacterial dispersal.

To study this systematically, we built a behavioral screen covering ~100 nematode-associated bacterial strains and characterized the distinct locomotor signatures each induces in worms. This collection now serves as a tool for dissecting how predation, associative learning, and community composition shift in response to worm foraging. In collaboration with the Yan lab (Yale), we are extending this framework to Tetrahymena protists — fellow microbe feeders — to ask whether these predator–prey dynamics are conserved across distantly related eukaryotes.
